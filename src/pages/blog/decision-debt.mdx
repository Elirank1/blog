You know about technical debt. You know about sleep debt. But if you’re running a one-person operation — consulting, freelancing, founding — there’s a quieter kind of debt eating your bandwidth: **decision debt.**

It’s not about making bad decisions. It’s about making decisions that never land anywhere.

## What Decision Debt Looks Like

Monday: you tell a client you’ll adjust the timeline. Tuesday: you decide to drop a feature from the proposal. Wednesday: you agree with your designer to change direction on the landing page. Thursday: you realize you forgot what you decided on Monday.

By Friday, you’re operating on a mix of memory, Slack scrollback, and anxiety. Nothing is written down. Nothing is canonical. You’re carrying every open decision in your head — and your head is not a database.

This is decision debt. And for solo operators, it compounds fast.

## Why It Hits Solo Operators Hardest

In a team, there’s usually someone whose job is to write things down. Meeting notes, Jira tickets, shared docs. You might hate the process, but at least decisions have a place to live.

When you’re solo, you are the CEO, the PM, the executor, and the note-taker. And the note-taker always loses.

The result: you revisit the same decision three times because you don’t trust your memory. You contradict yourself across channels without realizing. You feel busy but can’t explain what moved forward. You carry a low-grade mental load that never fully lifts.

Sound familiar?

## A Simple System That Actually Works

I built something for myself that changed this. It’s not an app. It’s not a tool. It’s a protocol — and it fits in a single markdown file.

This is exactly the problem [the Founder OS](/blog/founder-os/) was built to solve — but that system goes deeper, automating the logging and enforcement so you don’t have to hold the system together through discipline alone.

Here’s the setup. Takes 10 minutes.

### Step 1: Create Your Decision Log

One file. `decisions.md`. That’s it.

Rules: one line per decision. Date + what + context. If you can’t write it in one line, it’s not a decision yet — it’s still thinking. Write it within 24 hours or it doesn’t count. Unwritten decisions decay.

### Step 2: The Weekly Lock

Every Monday morning, spend 2 minutes answering three questions: What am I shipping this week? What am I NOT touching? What’s my biggest risk?

The “NOT touching” section is the most important part. It’s your immune system against scope creep — especially the kind that comes from inside your own head.

### Step 3: The 24-Hour Rule

This is the one that changed everything for me.

**Any decision you make has 24 hours to be written in your decision log. If it’s not there in 24 hours, it expires.**

No exceptions. No “I’ll remember.” If it’s worth deciding, it’s worth one line in a file.

What this does: it forces clarity — vague decisions can’t be written in one line. It creates an audit trail you can search. It kills revisiting. And it reduces mental load, because your brain stops trying to hold everything.

## What Changed For Me

I’ve been running this for a few weeks. The shift is subtle but real.

I stopped carrying decisions in my head. I stopped second-guessing choices I already made. I stopped the Sunday-night anxiety of “what did I forget this week?”

My decisions have a place to live. My weeks have a lock. And the mental quiet — that’s the part I didn’t expect.

## The Template

Copy this, create a `decisions.md` file wherever you keep notes, and start today. One line per decision, date and context. A weekly lock with what ships, what doesn’t, and the biggest risk. That’s it. No app required. No subscription. Just you, a file, and the discipline to write one line per decision.

The same principle applies to everything else in your workflow. Take manual processes that create bottlenecks — from decision logging to content creation — and ask: what would it look like to turn this [from manual to production line](/blog/ai-production-line/)?

The system that manages your decisions doesn’t need to be complex. It needs to be honest.
